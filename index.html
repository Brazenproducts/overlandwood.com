The Overlander's Firewood Challenge

Overlanding campfire wood presents a logistics problem that car campers at developed campgrounds do not face. Your destination may have zero available local wood (desert, alpine), abundant wood you cannot legally collect (protected wilderness), or theoretically available wood that is actually too wet or unsuitable to burn. Planning before you leave is essential.

What to Carry: The Overlanding Firewood Kit

For a 3 to 4 day overland trip planning 4 to 5 campfires: carry approximately 25 to 35 pounds of kiln-dried hardwood plus a bundle of fatwood fire starters. This provides reliable campfire fuel regardless of local wood availability or condition. Pack in a mesh bag or sealed tote to prevent debris.

Kiln-dried juniper or hickory bundles from Amazon are ideal for overlanding because they are aromatic, predictable, and available in convenient weights for pre-trip ordering. Cedar splits are lighter weight per volume but deliver lower BTU. For serious cold-weather camping where fire is a primary heat source, increase to 40 to 50 pounds of kiln-dried oak.

Where and When You Can Legally Forage

Many National Forests and BLM areas permit collection of dead and down wood for personal campfire use without a permit. Key rules: wood must be dead and already on the ground, not cut from standing dead trees in most jurisdictions. Collection is typically limited to personal use quantities (less than what fits in a pickup bed). Always check current regulations with the specific field office before collecting.

Prohibited areas: all National Parks prohibit wood collection. Most wilderness areas prohibit or heavily restrict wood fires and collection. State parks vary significantly by state. High-use recreation areas near popular trails often restrict collection to prevent depletion. When in doubt, carry your own and leave the local wood for wildlife habitat.

Fire Safety on Trail: Non-Negotiable Rules

Desert and mountain environments amplify fire risk beyond what most campers encounter at developed campgrounds. Always check InciWeb, fire.airnow.gov, and the specific land management agency for current fire restrictions at your destination before departure. Fire restrictions change rapidly based on weather conditions.

Essential field safety: always use an established fire ring if one exists. If not, dig a shallow pit or use a portable fire pan to prevent ground fires from spreading through root systems. Keep fire small and under control, never above 2 feet high in dry conditions. Have at least one gallon of water available. Drown, stir, drown again before sleeping.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the most common questions about campfire wood overlanding, updated based on current research and reader feedback.

How much firewood should I pack for an overlanding trip?

For 3 to 4 days with evening campfires: 25 to 35 pounds of kiln-dried hardwood plus fatwood starters. For 5 to 7 days or cold-weather trips where fire serves as heat: 40 to 60 pounds. This seems like a lot but kiln-dried wood is compact and dense.

Can I collect firewood on BLM land while overlanding?

Often yes, but regulations vary by specific BLM field office and area. Most areas permit collection of dead and down wood for personal use. Always check with the local BLM office or blm.gov for the specific area. Some popular recreation areas restrict all collection.

What is the best firewood to carry in a truck or overlanding rig?

Kiln-dried juniper or hickory bundles are ideal. They are aromatic (pleasant campfire smell), burn hot, and come in pre-measured bundles that are easy to manage. For maximum heat in cold conditions, kiln-dried white oak delivers the best BTU per pound of any commonly available product.

How do I start a campfire reliably in wet or windy conditions?

Use fatwood fire starters combined with small dry kindling. Fatwood ignites reliably in wet conditions because its resin content burns regardless of surface moisture. Create a windbreak with rocks or your vehicle if conditions are windy. A small wind-proof lighter is more reliable than matches in field conditions.

Can I buy firewood near popular overlanding destinations?

Many small towns near popular overland destinations sell firewood at gas stations and general stores. Quality and dryness is variable. If driving through a town the day before your destination, check for local firewood sellers. Do not count on it as your primary source.

What fire-starting gear should be in every overland kit?

Mandatory: waterproof lighter (BIC is reliable), fatwood fire starters, small dry tinder in a sealed container. Optional but useful: ferro rod backup, fire paste or wax cubes for wind resistance, fire bellows for oxygen control. Keep fire starting gear in one accessible grab bag, not buried in gear.

Is it safe to have a campfire in a vehicle fire pan?

Yes. Portable fire pans (essentially a metal bowl or tray) are the responsible choice in pristine areas without established fire rings. They prevent ground contact, protect vegetation, and make cleanup and transport of ashes easy. Most wilderness areas that permit fires require or strongly recommend fire pans.

How do I know if fire is allowed at my overlanding destination?

Check InciWeb at inciweb.nwcg.gov for current fire restrictions, which are updated daily during fire season. Also check the specific land management agency website (BLM, USFS, state parks) for your area. Apps like CalTopo and OnX often include current fire restriction overlays.
